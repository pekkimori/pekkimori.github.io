## Introduction

In the last four months, I went through two mental breakdowns. To give some context:

- I do almost all of my work on a computer, and since a few months ago, I didn't have my own; I'd use loaners, and I had to return my usual loaner before moving to Tokyo. 

- At the beginning of the Fall semester, my mom went through a hysterectomy, with a chance of her having a tumour, so she couldn't work for a while. 

- I had multiple projects to work on at the same time, and as I always had financial struggles, I didn't have time or money to sustain myself or buy a laptop for my school/work. 

- I fought with my ex, and things ended up worse than before. 

- I passed out from burnout before a deadline during assignment week, so I couldn't submit a few assignments. 

- I got a severe case of the flu, which left me in bed for two weeks; I had absences that my university didn't excuse, therefore, my grades went downhill. 

All that happened in the first two to three months, and there is much more to say, things that happened afterwards, which I will address later in this post.

Now, this is not about me complaining about the bad things that happened to me (at least partially). I'll first tell my story, but my main goal here is to show how these moments drastically changed who I am, plus that there is something to learn here, in case someone who is also going through a lot may read this, giving them some direction and hope that things will get better.

## Who I was during the first crisis

## The second crisis

Suddenly, the concerns that were lurking around caught me.
That had never happened to me, and I didn't know what to do. My anxiety levels went up again, and my conditions worsened again.

She distanced herself, taking long to reply, and apathetically when doing so. Things were out of my control again, and soon, I felt powerless, for not being able to help or make her feel better, all I could do was send her messages to check if she was eating and sleeping; and of course, make myself available for whatever she needed. 

We spent one month in this dynamic, the only meaningful messages I got were: "I feel guilty for making you wait", and "Thank you for not abandoning me." I felt like things were going to break again.

Then, we finally met to talk properly.

In short, she didn't have the energy or time for a relationship anymore, even though she wanted to be with me. She felt guilty of only interacting with me when she was in the mood.

I thought ending it was perfectly reasonable, even though it was not exactly what I wanted ‚Äì I wanted to be there for her when she needed the most, when she was vulnerable and at her lowest. I just hoped that decision would be the best for her. So we decided it was better to break up. 

She left me with a request: "Please, just don't forget me."

Of course, I'm not a robot, even though rationally it was all reasonable, I was emotionally destroyed:

>You know, things don't always work out or happen the way we expect, and that uncertainty is one of life's most basic certainties. I made the decision to stay with her, but unfortunately, the universe decided it wasn't meant to be right now. ## Conclusion

After everything that happened, I felt like a new person. Broken and reconstructed.

I'm still not able to explain why, but my social anxiety almost vanished. It felt like people's actions and opinions didn't truly reflect who I was; I didn't trust their words as much anymore. I had lots of motivation to follow through with my projects, work, and school; they all felt so meaningful and purposeful, and I have found a new reason to move forward. I no longer feel unhappy with my personal life; I actually have so many amazing people in my life, so many good things overflowing and coming. I just never gave the proper attention to the things I achieved.

One year ago, I was asked this question: "Define who you are." My answer was something along the lines of "Nerd, weird, clumsy." I don't even know why I said clumsy. I'd try to bullshit something in interviews, about how I cared deeply about something beautiful at the front, which I didn't really care that much about. I just moved forward because I wanted to put my family out of that ?!?!?! hole in Brazil, live a comfortable life, and have fun.

But today I can say that I looked at that black hole and said: "I'll illuminate this galaxy, it doesn't matter how much light you drain!", that I stared at the demon's face and said: "I am going after you. I'll be the one out there to get you." I know it can be naive or dumb, but I don't want to live in a world where that kind of suffering exists. I won't be part of that gruesome feedback loop; I want to break it. I want to live in a world full of love, joy, kindness, truth, hope, meaning, integrity, growth... I despise the fact that the universe can be this unfair, cruel, and stupid, and I won't be able to live with myself if I don't do anything about it. The universe didn't make me the comet out of nowhere; my mom and dad just wanted me to be good, study, and then do whatever I wanted to.     Kant asserts that ethical decisions are based on the categorical imperative rather than the hypothetical imperative.
    
    An imperative means something that tells you what to do, for example: "Go touch some grass" is in the imperative form.
    
    Hypothetical here means in unique, specific scenarios or goals; therefore, we can say that the hypothetical imperative is what you should do in relation to certain occasions in life, for example: "you should go to the gym if you want to get stronger", "you should read more if you want to get smarter", "you should diet if you want to lose weight".
    
    Categorical here means that it is independent of the scenario; therefore, a categorical imperative is something that you should do, independent of the case, goal, person, outcome, whatever; the goodness is intrinsic in the action.
