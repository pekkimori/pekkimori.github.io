## Preface.

You know, writing takes a long time. But living "well" is far too important a subject for me to keep putting off.

It started when I went through some rough patches on my own. There was nobody to give me the answer. And even if I could call my parents, knock on my friend's door, or ask a stranger on the internet—I felt that none of it would bring me what I needed. That's when I realized: the answer wasn't outside. It was inside.

The sculpture was already there. I just had to carve it.

So I started writing. Not because I already knew what I think, but because writing is my way of finding out. I have an intuitive understanding—I feel it in the decisions I make, in the things that move me, in the way I see the world. And I want to understand it better.

Therefore, this text is the result of that. It's my understanding of the world and, from there, a perspective on how to live "well" (with emphasis on the quotation marks and emphasis on the emphasis).

My goal is to talk about all of this in a fun, educational, and clear way. Philosophy has this problem: you start searching for reason and, halfway through, you get lost in your own terms and metaphors. I want a text that is rich, yet simple. Brutal, yet human.

I'm going to talk about three core values: truth, energy, and love.[^1] But I won't get to them directly. First, I need to show where they come from—after all, I don't want you to accept what I write just because I wrote it. I want you to think alongside me, to disagree if you disagree, and to one day send me a message telling me what you thought. Maybe you'll change my perspective too.

At the end, I'll connect the three values and show how they apply to real life—and what happens when we live without them. I'll bring theories from different fields, ancient and modern thoughts, and try to show that very different people, thinking in very different ways, can arrive at a shared and elevated principle when we genuinely try to be better.

But before anything else, I have an important question for you: *how should you live and why?* Don't answer it now. Hold onto it.

## Premises.

Everything begins with three premises. They are the foundation. Without them, anything I build afterward has nothing to stand on.

• • •

### *Everything in the universe is change or contrast.*

You can see this by thinking about academic fields. In physics, we create models—like the ones that predict the motion of a ball when kicked by the young Pelé. It's the study of how the ball's position varies over time. And you can see that we're concerned with the change in the ball's position, in contrast with time.

Another example is the study of language. We study how the meanings of words change from generation to generation and also compare how Brazilian Portuguese varies from region to region, even in relation to European Portuguese. Saussure, the father of modern linguistics, used to say that words don't have meaning on their own: they only mean something in contrast with other words. What is hot without cold, north without south, beautiful without ugly?

If you stop to think long enough, you'll see that everything—our understanding of everything—is based on change and contrast. There is no static state. A point is imaginary and abstract. Reality is constant change. We only see color through the movement of electromagnetic waves. We only distinguish an apple from an orange because they differ in size, shape, taste, smell, and sound. Something only exists if there was change. The act of painting only exists if it changed the color of the paper; otherwise, it didn't exist. The painting only exists because we see several different colors organized on paper; otherwise, it doesn't exist. And if everything were the same forever, in truth, nothing would ever exist; it would be a blank, a void, nonexistent, and infinite. Heraclitus already said this in Ancient Greece: *panta rhei*—everything flows. You don't step into the same river twice, because the water has changed and so have you. And Hegel took this idea and built an entire logic from it, through the dialectic: reality is not static; it moves through contradictions. Thesis (being), antithesis (non-being), synthesis (becoming).

And there's a consequence of this that's worth pausing to consider. If everything is relation, if everything only exists in contrast with something else, then nothing exists in isolation. Presence only makes sense in relation to absence. The individual only makes sense in relation to society. An object only makes sense within a universe that contains it and contrasts it with everything else. Everything in the universe is an interconnected system—and it is the connections that confer meaning. A node, alone and disconnected, is not a node; it is simply nothing.

Aristotle already said that the human being is a *zoon politikon*—a political animal, a being of relationships. Not in the sense that we like politics (though we do, even without knowing it), but in the sense that outside of community, outside of relationship with others, we are not fully human. A completely isolated man, he said, is either a beast or a god. Hegel also builds on this, saying that self-consciousness itself only arises in the encounter with the other. You only know you exist because there is something that is not you. Identity is not something you have alone; it is something that emerges from difference.

And this isn't just in philosophy. Many contemporary analyses speak of complex systems, of networks in which the behavior of each element depends on all the others. Biology speaks of ecosystems where the loss of one species throws the whole thing off balance. Emergent properties. Life emerges from molecules that, individually, are not alive, but together become an abstract concept that makes sense. A single ant is almost nothing: just a simple, limited, and predictable behavior. A colony of ants builds sophisticated architectural structures, solves complex problems, and adapts to the environment in ways no individual ant could plan. Meaning always emerges as an abstraction of relationship, never of isolation. And if you remove the connections, you don't get a smaller system; you get the collapse of the entire meaning, and, consequently, the abstraction breaks apart.

This has an implication: understanding what something is *not* is actually just as important as understanding what it is. Darkness teaches you about light. Pain teaches you about pleasure. Grief teaches you about love. Not because the negative is good in itself—but because without it, the positive is invisible, undifferentiated, nonexistent. That's why when I talk about truth, energy, love, and other definitions, I'll also talk about their opposites. You can't understand one without its relationship to the other.

### *The universe is chaotic.*

What I mean by this is that, in practice, the world is not deterministic. We don't have a law that predicts with exact precision what will happen in the future. And honestly, we don't have such a law for micro scales, or macro scales, or medium scales, or any scale at all. We are, in reality, lost. That's where the chaos of the universe comes from; it is essentially unpredictable from the human point of view. Nassim Taleb spent his entire life talking about this: that the world is dominated by unpredictable events called black swans, and that most people get burned because they insist on thinking the future will look like the past.

Physics has a name for the natural tendency of the universe: entropy. The second law of thermodynamics says that, in a closed system, disorder only increases. Leave a cup of coffee on the table, and it cools down. Leave a house unattended, and it rots. Leave a relationship without care, and it disappears. Order is not the natural state of things; it is the exception. It demands energy, intention, and continuous effort. Chaos is the default.

And this changes everything when we think about values. If entropy is the natural direction of the universe, then choosing to grow, to build meaning, to love—these are not neutral acts. They are acts of resistance. They go against the current. Which doesn't mean they're impossible. On the contrary—the observer affects the nature of matter; opening the box is what determines whether the cat is alive or dead.

Looking at the quantum level, we can thank our dear Bohr and Heisenberg for introducing the perspective that the world is uncertain. You cannot simultaneously know the position and momentum of a particle with precision; in other words, certain properties are mutually exclusive by nature, and therefore, the observer and the observed system are inseparable. In the end, reality is a superposition of probabilities that only collapses into a real, defined state through the act of observation. Which is curious, because it circles back to our first premise: something (the observed system) only exists with its complement (the observer).

From this emerges the notion of free will and our responsibility to be extraordinarily improbable. The universe is chaotic by nature, but those who have managed to create meaning, to see a pattern, to generate knowledge about what doesn't make sense, and to persevere in the face of confusion have an advantage when it comes to living: they can change the world and, potentially, be the agent that defines the system and saves the kitten.

And look, I know you can argue: how can we claim all this if we don't even know the truth about the universe yet? It's a paradox: if the universe is uncertain, we can't be certain that it's uncertain, and there's no certainty behind it. Maybe Bohr is wrong, and the world is deterministic; maybe there is a law that governs everything, whether physical or religious, and your destiny is predefined. Maybe Einstein was right, again. *"Gott würfelt nicht,"* as he would say. And I agree with you: 99.99% possible. But that thought isn't very useful in practice; after all, you don't know the absolute law and, therefore, you'll have to act in uncertainty anyway. That's exactly why this premise only makes complete sense together with the one that follows.

• • •

### *The human being is limited.*

We can create dreams, we can improve, we can do incredible things working as a group, but one certainty is that we have limits. Nobody here is any kind of god, and that's a fact. If you're in pure isolation, you're most likely just a beast, driven by instinct and impulse. Period. Being perfect implies knowing and following an absolute law. That's why we're not going to be perfect; nobody is perfect, and that's why there's no point in trying to be perfect. But don't be fooled: trying to be perfect is different from trying to be better. Trying to be better is absolutely worth it. After all, if we've improved in life, we can measure it (because we can compare how we are today with what we were yesterday), but we can't measure how close we are to 100% (because we don't know what 100% is, if it even exists). Hence the danger of blindly believing in ideals and, at the same time, of reaching them and realizing they were never ideals at all—you've probably heard "never meet your heroes."

We can't understand many things at once. It's genuinely hard. I mean, trying to understand the purpose of the universe—what a brutally difficult subject. What is time? Holy hell, what a mind-bending concept. And now you ask me: do we have an answer? I answer: there is no answer yet; I haven't found one, I don't know. If you do, tell me. Kant spent his entire life mapping the limits of human reason and concluded that we will never know the thing-in-itself, only our perception of it. Wittgenstein was even more direct: "The limits of my language are the limits of my world." If you don't have words to describe something, you literally can't think properly about it. And Kahneman, Nobel Prize winner in Economics, spent decades cataloging the crooked mental shortcuts, heuristics, and biases that our brain uses—and showed that this is literally how our brain works: all wrong.

And there's an even deeper limitation we haven't even touched. To this day, we don't really know how matter gives rise to experience and free will. Think about it: your brain is a clump of neurons firing electrical signals. All physical, all measurable. And yet, somehow, from that emerges the sensation of seeing the blue of the sky, of missing grandma's rain-day cake, of having the subjective experience of being you. David Chalmers called this "the hard problem of consciousness"—hard not because it's just complicated, but because we don't even know where to begin answering it. Science explains very well how the brain processes information. It doesn't explain why that processing comes with an inner experience. And if we don't understand how the observer's own consciousness works, imagine understanding the system of the world out there.

Korzybski, a linguist of the 20th century, had a simple phrase that sums it all up: *"The map is not the territory."* The map is useful.[^2] The map helps you not get lost. But the map will never be the real forest (with the smell of wet earth, the sound of rain, the damn mosquito in your ear). We live by maps. Language is a map. Science is a map. Memory is a map—and one full of edits, cuts, and distortions we don't even notice. All understanding is representation, and all representation is simplification. The point is not to escape the map, because that's impossible. The point is to remember and accept that you're using one.

## Three premises. One conclusion.

The universe is made of change and contrast; nothing exists alone; everything depends on relationships to have meaning, and the properties of reality emerge precisely from those connections. This universe is chaotic and unpredictable; entropy is the default, order is the exception, and no law guarantees us what comes next. And we—the beings who try to understand all of this and have the power to alter reality—are profoundly limited: by our senses, by our language, by our biases, by a consciousness we don't even properly understand, living inside maps that, at every moment, we mistake for the territory.

*And yet we must act. Otherwise, we would deny our existence—we would die.*

This is the human condition. And it is precisely from this existential condition that the need for a structure or hierarchy of values arises. After all, the universe is neither good nor bad; it simply is. Morality, good and evil, right and wrong are not written in the stars. We invent them. Kierkegaard and his existentialism say that this freedom to create is truly frightening—the so-called "dizziness of freedom," the vertigo of realizing that you could be anything, follow any path, believe in anything. It's agonizing.

Nietzsche said "God is dead" not to be edgy, but to say that we can no longer outsource our values to an external source—and moreover: if you don't define your values, the void defines them for you, and that's dangerous. Ivan Karamazov, a character from Dostoevsky's *The Brothers Karamazov*, argued that "if God does not exist, everything is permitted." My answer is: permitted, perhaps. But not everything makes us grow. Not everything makes us more human. And that is exactly why choosing a structure or hierarchy of values and truly committing to it is one of the most courageous and necessary acts that exists.

What happens is that defining what is good and what is evil in abstract terms is easy: good is what we should do, evil is the opposite, what we should not do. It's essentially a practical and arbitrary tool for making decisions. The problem arises when we have to draw the line that separates good from evil—imagine having a board with black and white dots scattered around and trying to trace a single line that separates them. But the worst part is when you find the tablecloth-gray dot and have to justify whether it's black or white. Immediately, you realize that the error isn't in the answer, but in the question. It was never about black or white.

So this is where I place my bet: our current goal is not to distinguish right from wrong; it's to throw that concept in the trash. Life goes *beyond good and evil.* Moreover, there are situations that have neither a right nor a wrong—only different options. Therefore, we must create a new model to guide our lives, one with many shades of color springing from the three premises; one that makes us care and act in the face of the changes happening around us; that helps us make sense of the chaos; that draws us away from our limitations toward a fuller version of what we can be; that makes us capable of fulfilling our responsibility to be ever more extraordinary. And thus, naturally, we'll arrive at better answers and choices, finally painting the world in our own colors.

Therefore, we don't draw a fixed, immutable line, but rather a living line that adapts, identifies the gradient, and doesn't unfairly or easily ostracize. It's an essentially asymptotic definition—one we refine infinitely but never reach the final point. And that's okay. If you remember your calculus classes, it's easier to understand: it's not about getting there; it's about getting closer.

And, analogous to red, yellow, and blue, three values emerge as spectrums:

**Truth** — the confrontation with reality, no matter how hard it is to face or understand.

**Energy** — the force applied against entropy, the act of resisting chaos.

**Love** — the compass that decides how energy is consolidated and which truths to prioritize.

They are not definitive answers for "good" and "evil." But they are the path to seeing, interpreting, and evaluating a multitude of scenarios, as well as how to get ever closer to the answer to the most important question an individual can ask themselves:

*"Given you, how should you live?"*

To the incredible people around me—not just literally, but also metaphorically, rhetorically, poetically, theoretically, and in every other fancy way imaginable. To myself, because it wasn't easy getting to where I am. And to my white cat, Yuki!*
