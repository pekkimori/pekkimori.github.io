## **Preface**

You know, writing takes a long time. But living "well" is too important a subject for me to keep putting off.

This started when I went through some rough patches on my own. There was no one to give me the answer. And even if I could call my parents, knock on my friend's door, or ask a stranger on the internet — I felt like none of that would bring what I needed. That's when I realized: the answer wasn't out there. It was inside.

The sculpture was already there. I just had to carve it out.

So I started writing. Not because I already knew what I think, but because writing is how I figure it out. I have an intuitive understanding — I feel it in the decisions I make, in the things that move me, in the way I see the world. And I want to understand it better.

So this text is the result of that. It's my understanding of the world and, from there, a perspective on how to live "well" (with heavy emphasis on the quotation marks and the emphasis).

My goal is to talk about all of this in a fun, educational, and clear way. Philosophy has this problem: you start searching for reason and, halfway through, get lost in your own terms and metaphors.[^1] I want a text that's rich but simple. Brutal but human.

I'm going to talk about three core values: truth, energy, and love. But I won't get to them directly. First, I need to show where they come from — after all, I don't want you to accept what I write just because I wrote it. I want you to think along with me, disagree if you disagree, and maybe one day send me a message telling me what you thought. Maybe you'll change my perspective too.

In the end, I'll connect the three values and show how they apply to real life — and what happens when we live without them. I'll bring in theories from different fields, ancient and modern thought, and try to show that very different people, thinking in very different ways, can arrive at a common and elevated principle when we genuinely try to be better. Also, if it sparks your interest, feel free to dive deeper into the books recommended in the footnotes!

But before anything else, I have an important question for you: _how should you live and why?_ Don't answer it now. Hold onto it.

## **Introduction**

Everything begins with three premises. From them, we'll derive three values.

• • •

### _**The universe is change and contrast**_

You can see this by thinking about academic studies. In physics, we create models — like the ones that predict the motion of a ball when kicked by young _Pelé_. It's the study of how the ball's position changes over time. And you can see that what we care about is the change in the ball's position, in contrast with time.

Another example is the study of language. We study how words change from generation to generation: your grandfather saying someone is "swell" and "keen," and you saying they're "mid" and "giving nothing." We also compare how English varies from region to region — soda, pop, or coke; sneakers, tennis shoes, or trainers; sub, hoagie, or hero — and even between American and British English, innit? _Saussure_, the father of modern linguistics, said that words don't have meaning on their own: they only mean something in contrast with other words.[^2] What is hot without cold, north without south, beautiful without ugly, Batman without Robin, me without you?

If you stop and think hard enough, you'll see that everything — our understanding of everything — is based on change and contrast. There is no static state. A point is imaginary and abstract. Reality is constant change. We only see color through the movement of electromagnetic waves. We only differentiate an apple from an orange because they have different shape, taste, smell, sound, etc. The act of painting only exists if it changed the color of the paper; otherwise, nothing was painted. A painting only exists because we see various different colors organized on the paper; otherwise, there's no painting. If everything were the same forever, in truth, nothing would ever exist — it would be blank, a void, nonexistent and infinite. _Heraclitus_ said this back in Ancient Greece: _panta rhei_, everything flows.[^3] You never step into the same river twice, because the water has changed and so have you.

And there's a consequence of this worth pausing to consider. If everything only exists in relation to the change of one variable _x_ in contrast with another variable _y_, then nothing exists in isolation. Presence only makes sense in relation to absence. The individual only makes sense in relation to society. An object only makes sense within a universe that contains it and contrasts it with everything else. Everything in the universe is an interconnected system — and it's those connections that confer meaning. A node, alone and disconnected, is not a node; it's simply nothing.

_Aristotle_ already said that human beings are _zoon politikon_ — political animals, beings of relationships.[^4] Not in the sense that we like politics (though we do, even without knowing it), but in the sense that outside the community, outside relationship with others, we're not fully human. A completely isolated man, he said, is either a beast or a god. _Hegel_ also builds on this, saying that self-consciousness itself only arises in the encounter with the other.[^5] You only know you exist because there's something that isn't you. Identity isn't something you have alone; it's something that emerges from difference. The rich only perceive their privilege in the encounter with poverty, and the same applies in reverse — the poor only perceive their misery in the encounter with wealth. Give a kid one marshmallow for free and they'll be happy; then show them you gave their sibling ten, and instantly they'll feel cheated.

And this isn't just in philosophy. Many contemporary analyses speak of complex systems, of networks where the behavior of each element depends on all the others.[^6] Biology speaks of ecosystems where the loss of one species destabilizes everything else. Kill all the bees and humanity lasts only four more years: if you remove the bees, pollination simply stops, preventing the reproduction of flora; without greenery to sustain life, the animals disappear and, inevitably, the human race itself marches toward its end. Emergent properties.[^7] Life emerges from molecules that, individually, fall apart, but together create proteins, DNA, and become an abstract concept that makes sense.

A single ant is almost nothing: it just wanders around in circles. A colony of ants builds sophisticated architectural structures for you to step on barefoot and walk away covered in bites, solves complex problems to acquire the sugar hidden inside a plastic bag, in a cardboard box, in a drawer, inside four concrete walls, and adapts to the environment in ways no individual ant could plan — building nests even inside your ceiling. Meaning always emerges as an abstraction from relationship, never from isolation. And if you remove the connections, you don't get a smaller system; the entire meaning collapses and, consequently, the abstraction breaks apart.

This also has another implication: understanding what something is _not_ is actually just as important as understanding what it _is_. Darkness teaches you about light. Pain teaches you about pleasure. Grief teaches you about love. Not because the negative is good in itself — but because without it, the positive is invisible, undifferentiated, nonexistent. That's why when I talk about truth, energy, love, and other definitions, I'll also talk about their opposites. You can't understand one without its relationship to the other.

### _**Everything is chaotic**_

What I mean by this is that, in practice, the world is not deterministic. We don't have a law that predicts with exact precision the consequence of an action, how things will unfold, what property emerges from the relationship between two things, or how one relates to the other. Maybe TikTok has the answer for why your relationship didn't work out — next time, try dating someone who loves the _Fast and Furious_ franchise, because they're the truly loyal ones who'll never get tired of you, since they watch the same thing 10 times and lose their minds every time. Makes sense, right?

Honestly, we don't have that law for any scale; we don't understand atoms, people, or the universe.[^8] We're basically lost in reality, and everything seems a little random at the end of the day. This is where the chaos of the universe comes from; it's essentially unpredictable and probabilistic. _Nassim Taleb_ talks about this: the world is dominated by unpredictable events called black swans, and most people get burned because they insist on thinking the future will be just like the past.[^9]

Physics has a name for this natural tendency of the universe: entropy. The second law of thermodynamics says that in a closed system, disorder only increases.[^10] Leave a cup of coffee on the table and it gets cold. Leave a house unattended and it rots. Leave a relationship without attention and it disappears. Order is not the natural state of things; it's the exception. It requires energy, intention, and continuous effort. Chaos is the universe's default setting.

And this changes everything when we think about values. If entropy is the natural direction of the universe and of time, then choosing to grow, to build meaning, to find patterns, to organize, to truly love — these are not neutral acts. They're acts of resistance. They go against the current. Which doesn't mean they're impossible. On the contrary, the observer affects the nature of matter; opening the box is what determines whether the cat is alive or dead.

Looking at the quantum level, we can thank our dear _Bohr_ and _Heisenberg_ for introducing us to the view that the world is uncertain.[^11] You can't simultaneously know the position and momentum of a particle with precision; in other words, certain properties are mutually exclusive by nature and, therefore, the observer and the observed system are inseparable. In the end, reality is a superposition of probabilities that only collapses into a real, defined state with the act of observation — of registering something. Which is curious, because it circles back to our first premise: something (the observed system) only exists with its complement (the observer). From this arises the notion of free will and our responsibility to be extraordinarily improbable. The universe is chaotic by nature, but those who manage to grow, build meaning, find patterns, organize, truly love, make sense of what doesn't make sense, and persevere in the face of confusion have a hell of an advantage when it comes to living: they can change the world and, potentially, be the agent that defines the system and saves the cat.

And look, one of the arguments against this is: how can you assert all this if we don't even know the truth about the universe yet? It's a paradox: if the universe is uncertain, we don't have the certainty that it's uncertain and that there isn't a certainty behind all of it. Maybe _Bohr_ is wrong and the world is deterministic; maybe there's a law governing everything, whether physical or religious, and your destiny is predefined. Maybe _Einstein_ was right, again. _"Gott würfelt nicht"_ — "God does not play dice," as he would say. And I agree with you: 99.99% possible, but this thought isn't very useful in practice; after all, you don't know the absolute law or what _God_ really means — it'd be on you to figure out which religion is the right one, so you don't end up in some hell by accident.

In the end, when you die, the god of Pastafarianism could show up and tell you: _"Yeah buddy, you prayed to the wrong god, man! Sorry, but according to our book, you didn't follow the 10 Condiments and you'll have to spend the rest of eternity eating birdseed. Our other 4 followers who knew and followed the one true truth get to enjoy spaghetti. Bon appétit, mon amour!"_ And so you'd have to act under uncertainty regardless. And well, that's exactly why this premise only makes sense together with the one that follows.

### _**Human beings are limited**_

We can dream, we can improve, we can do incredible things working as a group, but one certainty is that we have individual limits. The sky is not the limit, but nobody here is a god of any kind, and that's a fact. If you're in pure isolation, you're most likely just a beast, driven by instinct and impulse. Period. Being perfect implies knowing and following an absolute law. So, whether or not you believe one exists, we're not going to be perfect — and that's why there's no point in trying to be perfect. But don't be fooled: trying to be perfect is different from trying to be better. Trying to be better is absolutely worth it. After all, if we've improved in life, we can measure it _(because we can compare who we are today with who we were yesterday)_, but we can't measure how close we are to 100% _(because we don't know what 100% looks like, or if it even exists)_. Hence, the danger of blindly believing in ideals and, at the same time, reaching them and realizing they were never ideals — you've probably heard "never meet your heroes."

We can't understand many things at once. It's genuinely hard. I mean, trying to understand the purpose of the universe. Jesus, what a hard topic. What is time? Holy hell, what an impossibly difficult thing to grasp. And now you ask me: do we have an answer? I reply: no answer yet; I haven't found one, I don't know. If you do, tell me. _Kant_ spent his entire life mapping the limits of human reason and concluded that we can never know the thing-in-itself, only our perception of it.[^12] _Wittgenstein_ was even more direct: _"The limits of my language are the limits of my world."_ If you don't have words to describe something, you literally can't think properly about it.[^13] And _Kahneman_, Nobel Prize winner in Economics, spent decades cataloguing the crooked mental shortcuts, heuristics, and biases our brains use, and showed that this is literally how — all messed up — our brain works.[^14]

And there's an even deeper limitation we haven't even touched. To this day, we don't really understand how matter gives rise to experience and free will. Think about it: your brain is a pile of neurons firing electrical signals. All physical, all measurable. And yet, somehow, from that arises the sensation of seeing the blue of the sky, of missing your grandma's chocolate chip cookies, of having the subjective experience of being _you_. _David Chalmers_ called this _"the hard problem of consciousness"_ — hard not because it's complicated, but because we don't even know where to begin answering it.[^15] Science explains very well how the brain processes information. It doesn't explain why that processing comes with an inner experience. I don't know if you grasp just how dumb an individual can be. If we can't understand how the observer's own consciousness works, imagine understanding the system of the world out there.

_Korzybski_, a linguist of the 20th century, had a simple phrase that sums it all up: _"the map is not the territory."_ The map is useful.[^16] The map helps you not get lost in the forest. But the map will never be the actual forest _(with the smell of wet earth, the sound of rain, that damn mosquito buzzing in your ear)_. We live by maps. Language is a map. Science is a map. Memory is a map — and a map full of edits, cuts, and distortions we don't even notice. All understanding is representation, and all representation is simplification.[^17] The point isn't to escape the map — that's impossible. The point is to remember and accept that you're using one.

### _**The ethics of the human condition**_

The universe is made of change and contrast; nothing exists alone; everything depends on relationships to have meaning, and the properties of reality emerge precisely from those connections. This universe is chaotic and unpredictable; entropy is the default, order is the exception, and no law guarantees what comes next. And we, the beings who try to understand all of this and have the power to alter reality, are profoundly limited: by our senses, our language, our biases, by a consciousness we don't even properly understand, living by models we mistake for absolute truths.

_And yet we must act. Otherwise, we'd be denying our existence — collective suicide._

And here's the problem most people ignore: to act is to choose. To choose is to prioritize. To prioritize is to have criteria. To have criteria is to have values. There's no such thing as a neutral action. _Sartre_ hammered this until the nail begged him to stop: you can't _not_ choose.[^18] Standing still is a choice. Not voting is a vote. Saying nothing says a lot. The guy sitting on the couch watching the house burn down because he "doesn't want to get involved" made a choice — and a pretty cowardly one. Inaction is the action of someone who doesn't have the guts to put their name on it. And if every action is a choice, then every human being, like it or not, is already operating with a hierarchy of values — the question is whether it was consciously chosen or inherited on autopilot, like that Spotify playlist you never edited that still has songs that make you cringe.

This is where we need to separate two things that English — and most people — treat as synonyms: morality and ethics. Morality is the inherited code. It's the set of rules your social group handed you when you were born: _"don't lie,"_ _"respect your elders,"_ _"don't eat dessert before dinner,"_ _"don't wear white after Labor Day."_ Morality is like the operating system that came pre-installed on your computer — you didn't choose it, you didn't read the terms of service, but it's running. Ethics, on the other hand, is the act of installing Linux and asking: _why?_ Why does this rule exist? Whom does it serve? Does it still make sense in the world I live in? Ethics is the reverse engineering of morality.[^19]

And given everything we've said about the human condition, inheriting a morality without questioning it is like using your great-grandparent's map to navigate downtown Manhattan. The world has changed and keeps changing; the map doesn't even make sense anymore. _Nietzsche_, when he said _"God is dead,"_ was saying exactly this: the external source from which we got ready-made rules no longer works as a guarantee.[^20] Not because _God_ can't exist, but because outsourcing the responsibility for your values is just a sophisticated form of intellectual cowardice. Therefore, we're forced into ethics — into continuous reflection, a process that never reaches a final answer but improves with every step.

But now a pesky problem appears: if we need values, where do they come from? Metaethics studies exactly this and has at least three classic answers worth knowing to situate where we're standing.[^21]

The first is moral realism: good and evil exist independently of us, like the green apple we discussed. There are moral facts in the universe the same way there are physical facts — we just need to discover them. It's an elegant position, but it has the same problem as absolute truth _(which we'll see in the next chapter)_: if it exists, we don't have direct access to it. It's like saying there's a perfect restaurant somewhere in the universe, but nobody knows the address, the menu, or whether they take Venmo.

The second is constructivism: values are collectively built, like _Searle_'s institutional facts _(which we'll discuss further ahead)_. Money is nothing until everybody agrees it's something. Human rights don't exist in nature — they were invented, debated, written, and signed by people with big mustaches in smoky rooms. And they work because enough people synchronized their belief in them. This is the position that best fits our premises.

The third is expressivism: saying _"that's wrong"_ doesn't describe any fact about the world — it's like saying _"eww, gross."_ It's an emotional expression dressed up as a proposition.[^22] When you say _"that's bad behaviour,"_ the expressivist hears _"OMG... EWW, GROSS!"_ The problem is that if values are just emotions, any moral debate becomes a fan war — and we already have sports for that.

I don't need to settle this debate here _(nor could I — premise three, remember?)_. But it's important to be honest about where we're standing. What this text proposes is something close to a pragmatic constructivism: values don't fall from the sky, but they're not arbitrary either; they emerge from our human condition and the premises we've established, and they're tested against reality.[^23] They're constructed, but not invented on a whim. It's the difference between building a bridge with engineering and building a bridge with popsicle sticks and lots of hope.

And this is where the reason I propose three values instead of a single rule comes in. Just as in quantum physics we have mutually exclusive properties, I believe that when we talk about morality, we need multiple laws that complement each other to generate a whole. Because the history of philosophy has already tried to solve this with a silver bullet, and they all failed — not because they were bad, but because they were incomplete.[^24]

Consequentialism, from _Bentham_ and _Mill_, says: the right thing is what produces the most well-being for the greatest number of people.[^25] Sounds reasonable, until you realize that by this logic, if torturing one innocent person saved a thousand, that'd be fine. It's the logic of _"the ends justify the means"_ — and anyone who's watched Death Note knows where this ends: a guy with a magic notebook and a God complex deciding who lives and who dies because _"it's for the good of humanity."_[^26] Pure consequentialism is Energy without Truth or Love — it acts for the result but ignores the fact that we can't predict the future, and doesn't care who it steps on along the way (ultimately stepping on itself).

_Kant_'s deontology says the opposite: there are moral rules that must be followed regardless of the result.[^27] _"Don't lie, ever, under any circumstances, not even if the murderer asks where your friend is hiding."_ Kant literally defended this. It's the ethics of a robot following protocol while the building burns down. Deontology is Love without Truth or Energy — it thinks of self and other, but is too rigid for a society that doesn't read the manual and ignores the multiple possibilities of the universe.

Aristotle's virtue ethics proposes something else: the right thing is what a virtuous person would do. It's not about rules or results; it's about character. Flourishing in our _eudaimonia_ and excellence. The problem? Who defines and interprets that character. And historically, when someone decides they're the model of character, things go sideways fast — ask any cult leader or any "quantum life coach" with 200,000 Instagram followers. Virtue ethics is Truth without Energy and Love — it's grounded in observing what we are and in human nature, but it doesn't think about what lies beyond us and how to transcend our starting point, and despite considering the community as important for reaching our fullest version, it glosses over the system's influence on what we should be and actively are.

None of these theories, on its own, can handle the human condition we've described. A chaotic universe doesn't accept rigid rules. A limited being can't calculate all consequences. A community without shared principles becomes a jungle. That's why we need a synthesis — and that's exactly what the three values aim to do. Truth shows the paths. Energy makes you walk. Love decides which path. Separate, they're incomplete. Together, they're universal.

And before we get to the values, there's one last step: _why care?_[^28] You could accept everything I've said — the universe is chaotic, we're limited, we need ethics rather than inherited morality — and still ask: _"Okay, but why should I bother?"_

Levinas has an answer that's both simple and devastating: ethics doesn't begin in a code, a law, a book, or a commandment. It begins in the face of the other.[^29] When you look at another person — really look, not just a glance on the subway — something happens. You perceive that there's someone who, like you, suffers, desires, is afraid, feels hunger, has cried alone in the bathroom at least once. And that perception already creates a demand. It's a call that emerges from the relationship itself. Remember our first premise? Nothing exists in isolation. A node is only a node if it's connected. Well then: the face of the other is the most fundamental connection there is. To ignore it is to cut the thread that makes you human.

And if that seems too abstract, think of it this way: why do you hold the elevator door for a stranger, even when you're running late? Why do you feel a tightness when you see a child crying alone? Why does even the toughest guy in the office go quiet when someone says they lost a loved one? Why do you share your fries with your friends? It's not necessarily because someone taught you a rule. It's because the face of the other tells you something before any language or reasoning: _I exist, and it matters._ If you can't feel that, maybe you're already a monster.

This is the human condition. And it's precisely from this existential condition that the need for a structure or hierarchy of values arises. After all, the universe is neither good nor bad; it simply _is_. Morality, good and evil, right and wrong are not written in the stars. We invent them. _Kierkegaard_ says this freedom to create is terrifying indeed — the so-called "dizziness of freedom,"[^30] the vertigo of realizing you could be anything, follow any path, believe anything. And if you don't define your values, the void defines them for you, and that's very dangerous — entropy tends to turn us into monsters. If you, as a kid, don't go decide what you'll eat, your mom will decide for you. If you, upstanding citizen, don't choose who represents the people, the corrupt will choose for you. If you don't choose your future, the universe will choose for you. If you don't decide to be different from whoever hurt you, revenge will teach you to be just as rotten. And so you'll prostitute your purpose for someone else's, betraying yourself. Ivan Karamazov, a character in Dostoevsky's _The Brothers Karamazov_, argued that "if God does not exist, everything is permitted."[^31] My answer is: permitted, perhaps. But not everything makes us grow. Not everything makes us more human. And that's why choosing a structure or hierarchy of values and committing to it is one of the bravest and most necessary acts there is.

The thing is, defining good and evil in abstract terms is easy: good is what we should do, evil is the opposite — what we shouldn't do. It's essentially a practical and arbitrary tool for making decisions based on a binary contrast, just like your computer understanding things with 0s and 1s. The problem is when we have to draw the line separating good from evil — imagine having a board with black and white dots scattered across it and trying to draw a single line to separate them. But the worst part is when you find the grayish-beige dot and have to justify whether it's black or white; it's like finding the number π/5 and having to justify whether it's 0 or 1. Immediately, you realize the error isn't in the answer — it's in the question. It was never about black or white.

So here's where I plant my flag: our current goal isn't to distinguish right from wrong; it's to throw that concept in the trash. Life goes _beyond good and evil._ There are situations with no right or wrong, only different options. It's evolving from an ordinary computer to a quantum computer. Therefore, we must create a new ethics to guide our lives — one with many shades of color stemming from the three premises; one that makes us care and act in the face of the changes happening around us; that helps us make sense of the chaos; that moves us away from our limitations toward a fuller version of what we can be; that makes us capable of fulfilling our responsibility to be ever more extraordinary. And so, naturally, we'll arrive at better answers and choices, finally painting the world in our colors.

Therefore, we don't draw a fixed and immutable line, but rather a living line that adapts, identifies the gradient, and doesn't ostracize unfairly or easily. It's an essentially asymptotic definition — one we refine infinitely but never reach the endpoint. And that's okay. If you remember your calculus classes, it's easier to understand: it's not about getting there, it's about approaching.

Analogous to red, yellow, and blue — as spectrums — the values emerge:

**Truth** — that shows the paths; the lighthouse; the Euclidean space; the thesis.

**Energy** — that makes you walk; the wind; the magnitude of the vector; the antithesis.

**Love** — that decides which path; the rudder; the direction and sense; the synthesis.

And we'll see later that they're compasses that work together to guide us ever closer to the answer to the most important question anyone can ask themselves:[^32]

_**"Given you and me, how should we live?"**_

### _**Definition of truth**_

Now, the concept of truth is still being debated today. There are probably about 3 weirdos in individual dark rooms, studying philosophy at a public university, debating this right now. Good thing I'm perfectly the opposite — except for the fact that I'm also a weirdo. Anyway, I believe we can find a middle ground between the modern theories on the definition of truth.[^33]

The first definition is that of correspondence. There are things that carry truth — that can be true or false — such as things, sentences, propositions, or beliefs; we'll call them truth-bearers. Facts are pieces of reality — more specifically, objects and their properties. An object, like an apple, and a property, like its intentions, which could be evil. So a simple example would be: "The apple is evil." By this definition, if there's correspondence between the truth-bearer (our thing/sentence/proposition/belief) and reality, we say the sentence is true; otherwise, if in reality the apple is benign, the Snow White fans can relax, since the sentence is false.

The second definition is that of coherence. Here, truth doesn't need to match reality; it just needs to be nicely coherent within a system of beliefs. You can see that, by being detached from reality, you could say that an anime with a very coherent superpower system could be true — which, of course, it isn't necessarily: it's just an author's invention. In this way, individual beliefs are only partially true, because in this definition, truth itself is something more holistic. In this definition, there's a belief in a complete, whole, and absolute truth. It's a more idealistic definition.

The third definition is the pragmatic one. Here, truth is what works, what proves useful. That's why, from this perspective, when we "find" truth, we just stop looking for more, because we've found something sufficient for our context. So truth is more anchored in human practice than in an absolute, abstract truth. The biggest risk here is that by discarding the concept of a greater truth, we risk stopping too soon.

And finally, there's a theory that closely resembles the first correspondence definition, and we can call it Tarski's theory.[^34] It's something we always see in deductive logic. Biconditionals. Through language and semantics, we attach a reference to certain objects and a satisfaction condition regarding those objects' properties. For example, "the apple is happy" is true if and only if the apple is happy. And looking at what I just wrote, I look like an idiot. And that's exactly the point: it's supposed to be so obvious it hurts. Obviously, the apple is happy! Anyway, it's a way of formalizing the concept of truth in a logical system. This way, we can tie truth to the relationship between language and reality.

This is exactly why we need to be intentional, rigorous, and precise with our words. Tangentially, there's an interesting debate about whether the truth-bearer is a thing, sentence, proposition, or belief. The argument would be off-topic; however, truth can traverse all four and be translated between them. But since humans understand the world through language, when I refer to any of the truths, we'll do so through propositions: the meaning behind the words. (After all, there's no way for me to show you everything through a book full of letters; I have to write for you, and you have to make an effort to understand.)

Interestingly, we see evidence of the importance and real effect of language in neuroscience. Maryanne Wolf, in _Proust and the Squid_, shows that the human brain wasn't born to read — reading is a cultural technology that literally rewires our neural circuits and the different regions of the brain.[^35] Different writing systems produce different brain patterns, which means the language you use doesn't just describe how you see the world — it shapes the architecture with which you process it.

Lisa Feldman Barrett goes even deeper in _How Emotions Are Made_.[^36] She shows that the brain isn't a machine that merely reacts to the world — it's a prediction machine. At every moment, it generates hypotheses about what's going to happen, and the senses mainly serve to correct those predictions when they turn out wrong. And more importantly, what the brain uses to build these predictions are concepts — and those concepts are linguistic. Barrett demonstrates that people with richer emotional vocabularies don't just describe what they feel better — they also feel in more differentiated ways, because the brain has more tools to construct that experience. In other words: the vocabulary you have determines the granularity of the reality you can experience.

Wolf corroborates this, showing that deep reading activates not just decoding but also inference, analogy, and perspective-taking — what she calls "time to think beyond the text." In other words: developing better use of language expands your capacity for prediction and processing reality. This is exactly why arguing well, reading deeply, and constructing more precise sentences are literally ways of possessing more truth — not just describing it better, but seeing it more clearly.

Taking this perspective as our foundation, we can see that if we add just two elements to Tarski's theory and the correspondence definition — tying the sentence to a specific reference of reality and to a scope — we can unify all the definitions.

Bringing a new example, in which we say the apple is green, we can propose: "The apple, in objective reality, is green for me and my friend Gabriel." You're probably thinking: this guy is even dumber than I thought. And look, I'm stating the obvious in a way that seems redundant. In this sense, truth is tied to a specific reality. In this case, it's objective reality — that is, the system external to us, individuals and observers of the universe. Even if Gabriel were under the influence of substances and temporarily saw the apple as blue, this sentence would remain true, even though from his perspective it was a blue apple.

Furthermore, truth is also tied to a scope of individuals; that is, I'm not speaking to everyone, only to that specific group of observers of the universe who share the same model and the same reference for a concept. Presumably, the apple has no color at all for someone who is blind. Because for that blind person, when trying to analyze the objective reality of the apple's color, they'd probably have a different definition of color from ours, based on our intuitive definition of green. The same dilemma arises for someone approaching the apple at a speed close to that of light; in that case, due to the Doppler effect, the light waves would become gamma rays, invisible to the eye, which would probably give you cancer.

This example presents problems because we're not using the same model. And when I refer to the sentence "the apple, in objective reality, is green for me and my friend Gabriel," I'm assuming we use the same concept (model) of green and the same concept of apple. This is where the notion of pragmatics comes in: since we don't have access to absolute truth, whether it exists or not, we have to work from models and maps, and so we make practical, intuitive assumptions about these models and about what we really mean when we use our language. And so we'll call this scope — the domain of observers to which we refer.

To fit the coherence theory into all this, we can simply have truth tied to the infinite scope of a complete reality. Obviously, as you might imagine, I — a human being with a little peanut-sized brain — don't have access to this truth of infinite scope and complete reality, which may or may not exist, but we can play a mental, deductive game and imagine that it does. Therefore, we normally tie the concept of truth to a reality and a scope; thus, we can say its opposite is a false proposition — one that doesn't match the reference in that reality and that scope.

To illustrate the concept of false, we can invert our sentence: "The apple, in objective reality, is not green for me nor for my friend Gabriel." So if we look at objective reality and see a green apple — whether it's me or Gabriel — we'd have a false proposition. Note that we didn't change the scope or the reality we're referring to, because that would break our sentence and alter our definition of truth.

But given that the universe is chaotic and we never manage, in our human condition, to find complete sense in everything, when we achieve coherence, what we're actually doing is stopping the confrontation and the search for more truth, contenting ourselves with a scope and a reality. It's a practical coherence. It would be the choice — whether active or reactive, voluntary or involuntary — of not possessing certain truth (not knowing) or of not being in confrontation with reality (not seeking to know more, not expanding the scope or the domain of reality). In other words, ignorance. And so we can say that essentially all humans are ignorant of something, whether they want to be or not.

When we think in moral terms (considering truth as a value rather than a concept), we can't speak of opposites, because the concept transforms into a spectrum; however, we can speak of its absence: and in that case, it would be ignorance itself. It's important to pay attention here to the difference between the conceptual and the moral definitions. Note that whenever I speak of a moral definition, I'll be referring to a value that lives on a spectrum tied to our human condition.

And this is how, starting from the conceptual definition of truth, through syllogisms, logic, language, and understanding, we arrive at this moral definition: the pursuit of a greater truth. We can be people who manage to expand our maps and models and make claims that aren't restricted to a small scope or a merely subjective reality — such as, for example, your mere opinion.

But now we've created a new problem with this, which, honestly, was already a problem from the start.

### _**Definition of reality**_

Fortunately, this question has a well-established philosophical foundation.[^37] Realism has two central dimensions:

Existence — things exist. The moon exists; the sandwich made of two slices of bread, cream cheese, oregano, ham, cheese, lettuce, tomato, and pressed in a panini maker exists; the facts about it exist.

Independence — these things exist independently of what anyone thinks, says, or believes about them. The Earth isn't flat regardless of whether someone knows this, talks about it, or thinks otherwise. After all, the Earth is toroidal, like a donut.

Anti-realism can attack either dimension. You can say "those things don't exist" — like the innocent folks who believe lizard-people aliens don't exist — or "they exist, but not independently of us" — like Mark Zuckerberg.

From this, we can define what is objective, subjective, and complete:

Objective reality is that which exists independently of any specific observer — what you, Gabriel, and any other observer with the same model could verify. It's the domain of science, logic, and shareable facts.

Subjective reality is that which exists dependent on a specific observer — Gabriel's experience of seeing blue is real for him, even if it's "wrong" relative to objective reality. Pain, pleasure, the feeling of joy watching a raccoon eating grapes, the thrill of using Claude Code, firmly believing that gnomes steal your socks from the washing machine when you're not looking — these are real, but real for someone. They're not less real for it. They're real in a different way.

Complete reality would be the resultant of all observers, at all scales and dimensions. And here the quantum physics from our premises comes directly into play: if the act of observation affects reality, then the reality that exists is, in some sense, a function of who observes it. With a single observer, this seems problematic — would your particular view define reality as a whole? Not exactly. But with infinite observers, something interesting happens. The contribution of any individual observer becomes infinitesimally small relative to the whole. Complete reality depends on everyone — and precisely because of that, it doesn't depend on anyone in particular. We'll call this complete reality the universe.

It's from the universe that objective reality emerges. It's not that it's independent of observers in an absolute sense — it's that, in practice, the influence of any individual observer on it is negligible. It behaves as if it were independent of you. And when many observers, with the same models, converge — as in science, debate, shared verification — we're synchronizing perspectives (subjective realities) and approaching the complete reality and a broad understanding of the universe that takes shape as objectivity.

And all of this has a direct practical consequence. If objective reality acts "independently" of us, then ignoring it has a cost. You can build a very coherent subjective world — you can believe you're fine when you're not, that a relationship works when it doesn't, that your choices make sense when they don't. But objective reality doesn't disappear because you're not looking at it; after all, the influence of all the other observers of the universe — in other words, of the system itself — still exists.

This is exactly why truth, as a moral value, begins here. Confronting objective reality — even when it hurts, even when your map needs to be redrawn — is the only path to better maps.

But subjective reality also matters. It doesn't replace the objective, but it exerts force on it. When subjective realities converge, when multiple perspectives synchronize around something, they have the potential to alter objective reality itself. This is how science advances, how cultures change, how opinion becomes democracy, how the impossible becomes a little more possible. And when that synchrony is total, continuous, and growing, the line separating the subjective from the objective begins to blur — and we approach, asymptotically, complete reality.

In this definition, we can draw a direct parallel between the phenomenon of partial rewriting of objective reality and the concept of social construction. The philosopher John Searle distinguishes brute facts — the apple is green, the mountain has height — from institutional facts.[^38] Money is paper with ink. National borders are invisible from space. A company is a legal fiction. The mechanism Searle proposes is interesting: we assign functions to physical things through collective agreements — "this paper counts as money in this context." And when enough observers share this agreement, the fiction acquires the practical resistance of objective reality.

Berger and Luckmann called this objectivation: repeated, shared subjective practices that, over time, take on the appearance of facts about the world.[^39] The generations that inherit an institution didn't participate in its construction — they receive the finished product as if it were a truth. Harari popularized the same argument in _Sapiens_[^40]: for him, what distinguishes humans is the ability to believe in intersubjective fictions on a massive scale. Nations, rights, religions, laws — none of this exists in nature. Again, all of it exists because enough humans synchronized their belief in its existence. And this affects how you believe the universe really is; after all, it determines your first conception of it.

Thus, we can understand why the difference between the fiction of _My Little Pony_ and the fiction of Goldman Sachs is so grotesque: the latter is recognized by millions of people, can open a bank account and much more, while the former is merely a fantastical and questionable equine taste.

### _**Ignorance is bliss**_

The famous phrase: _"ignorance is bliss."_ Attributed to everyone and understood by almost no one, since the original text is quite different — and yet it ended up sparking a philosophical debate. I love when that happens. The worst part is when the bit people choose to extract is one-third of the complete sentence, on the second-to-last line of the text, right before a comma, in a 100-line poem.[^41] The full original sentence (discarding the other 98 lines of the poem) is: _"where ignorance is bliss, 'tis folly to be wise."_ It's clearly a direct critique of fools who live happily in their stupidity — our boys who don't want to leave Plato's cave, where the shadows on the wall are more comfortable than the light outside. But let's not take the sentence at face value; let's build our own argument using the tools we've constructed so far.

First, we need to define what we mean by bliss, and for the sake of a non-religious argument, we'll define it as: "that which brings about the good and happiness." In this way, since we've set aside the concept of good for now, let's focus on happiness. In that case, if you're a blissful fool, then yes, ignorance is a blessing — until you run into the "injustices" and chaos of life. Because if you live in the bubble (subjective reality) and refuse to explore outside it (objective reality), the moment the bubble bursts (a discrepancy between realities), you're lost and disoriented. Your happiness (coherence) remains as long as the bubble makes sense.

Now, if we use our moral model, ignorance is not a blessing — because objective reality doesn't make deals with you. It keeps being shaped by all the other observers of the universe while you look inward. And the bubble bursts for a reason. The world didn't stop happening while you pretended everything was fine. And when it bursts, you realize you were actually living in a round, fragile bubble; your entire reality breaks apart, and you have to build a new one.

However, this is inescapable: given our human condition, we live in these bubbles; they always burst and we always build new ones. That's why living, in general, requires a bit of faith in your experiences. Self-determination theory, from Deci and Ryan, shows us this in an interesting way: they argue that human beings need three basic psychological nutrients to function — competence, autonomy, and connection.[^42] (We'll draw a parallel between them and each of the values.) The focus of this chapter is on competence: the feeling that you can handle what comes. You act based on confidence that you're competent — even without being able to verify any of that with absolute precision in that moment. It's a kind of operational faith, like an informed bet based on the past and aimed at the future. You trust your models enough to act, knowing they're incomplete; that's why we need to adopt new mindsets capable of facing this obstacle productively.

The first mindset is simple: accepting that what you are or believe today is not what you'll be or believe forever. Carol Dweck spent years studying why some people crumble and throw a fit in the face of failure while others accept it and grow from it, and the answer she found has nothing to do with talent or intelligence — it has to do with mindset.[^43] People with a fixed mindset believe their abilities are permanent traits: either you're good at this or you're not. People with a growth mindset believe that abilities are just temporary states: you're _not yet_ good at this.

And here the connection with truth becomes clear. When you say "I'm not good at this" and treat it as a definitive fact, you're making a terrible mistake: you're confusing a current state, within a limited scope and a very specific time interval, with an absolute truth. You're blowing things way out of proportion, friend. In other words, you're choosing ignorance — perhaps out of fear of confronting the reality that you could be different, or out of mere habit. The growth mindset is, literally, getting used to not treating your current map as the territory.

But accepting that you can change doesn't solve the problem of how to act while the bubble hasn't burst yet — or worse, while it's bursting. This is where Stoicism comes in (a concept probably very different from what you think it is, thanks to the alpha males, lone wolves, and giga chads in the media).[^44] The Stoics, back in Greece and Rome, spent centuries developing what we'd now call an operating system for living in uncertainty. The central principle is the difference between what's within your control and what isn't. Within your control are your judgments, your intentions, your responses. Outside your control is practically everything else: the results of your actions, what others think of you, what the chaotic universe decides to do tomorrow, whether you'll have a wet dream tonight. Anchoring your stability in what's outside your control and beyond your limits is like building a house on the sand of the second law of thermodynamics: entropy will win and sink your house into the sand.

This doesn't mean just giving up or not caring about anything. In fact, the Stoics were profoundly active people — Marcus Aurelius governed an empire, Epictetus was a former slave who became an influential philosopher, Seneca advised emperors. What Stoicism proposes isn't giving up on results, but decoupling your integrity and identity from them. You act with everything you've got and give your best. You pursue truth rigorously and build the best map possible. And then, whatever the result, you don't break — because who you are — a badass human being — nobody can take. The Stoics called this virtue and said it was the only real good. I'd say it's the only thing that stays with you when the bubble bursts.

And here's where the two concepts meet. The growth mindset gives you a posture: what happened is just information, feedback; the universe isn't sentencing you to misery; you'll get better. Stoicism gives you strength: you don't need the universe to have been kinder to keep standing. Chin up! Together, they transform the inevitable bursting of the bubble into something you can walk through without shrinking into a broccoli in the fetal position eating ice cream in bed and crying yourself to sleep — you can look at what broke, understand why it broke, and in the future, build something better. This is what it truly means to pursue the truth.

### _**The fall into delusion**_

Lowering the level of abstraction, despite our pursuit of knowledge, by default our brain wants to reduce its energy expenditure — and constantly rebuilding a bubble or model demands a lot of energy. Festinger called the discomfort of holding two contradictory beliefs cognitive dissonance[^45] — and this discomfort is so aversive that the brain develops active strategies to reduce it. Not necessarily by seeking truth, but by restoring coherence. In Festinger's most famous experiment, a group that believed the world would end on a specific date, upon seeing that date pass without an apocalypse, didn't abandon the belief but instead reinforced it, concluding that their faith had saved the planet.[^46] Why not, right?

Kahneman shows that the brain operates in two systems: one fast and automatic, which processes most experience effortlessly; and one slow and deliberate, which costs a lot of energy and is only activated when necessary. Confirmation bias operates in the first — filtering new information through existing beliefs before the second has a chance to evaluate. Information that confirms what you already believe passes easily. Information that contradicts it meets resistance.

Haidt goes further and proposes that the brain works like a lawyer, not a scientist.[^47] A scientist starts with evidence and arrives at a conclusion. A lawyer starts with a conclusion and searches for evidence to support it. Reasoning, most of the time, is post-hoc: you have an intuition and then construct rational arguments to justify it. This is why debates rarely change minds — people naturally don't evaluate evidence; they defend positions they've already adopted.

The psychologist Peter Wason demonstrated this empirically: he asked people to test a logical rule; most chose the cards that would confirm the rule, not the ones that would falsify it.[^48] The natural impulse is to seek confirmation of what you think. This makes it easy for an individual to fall into truth's shadow: delusion.

Delusion is the mental state of someone who, through misinterpretation of reality, won't admit being wrong, even in the face of overwhelming evidence to the contrary. And if it persists and intensifies, it becomes a mental disturbance characterized by hallucinations, disorientation, confusion, and other symptoms. If we treated truth as a positive vector, ignorance would have zero magnitude, while delusion would be a negative vector — going against the pursuit of truth.

For this reason, delusion is more dangerous than ignorance, because while ignorance is neutral, delusion is an enemy — an active opposing force. It doesn't stay quiet. It hires lawyers, manufactures evidence, lies to itself, hides information from you, claims the Earth is flat, puts on a tinfoil hat, says it's God, says it's a lizard alien, builds a castle out of cardboard boxes in the public square and sues anyone who tries to enter. That's why fighting it requires more than goodwill or an open mind — it requires technique, struggle, and a healthy dose of patience. And moreover, it requires recognizing that there's a bit of delusion inside you.

After all, we're not always strong enough to face the truth. The human brain, as we've seen, wasn't built to seek reality — it was built to survive. And sometimes, surviving in our natural context means looking the other way and pretending you didn't see. We're fight or flight, and flight takes many forms: the story you tell about why the relationship didn't work out, the excuse you invent to justify keeping that anime body pillow in your closet even though you never use it and it's gathering dust, the narrative you construct so you don't have to change. Delusion starts with a small escape from reality. It's the immediate result of weakness.

Neuroscientifically, for you, everything you perceive is everything that exists. Thus, recognizing delusion in yourself is the first step toward intellectual courage. It's harder to see it in ourselves than in others, which is why the fight always starts from the inside out.

The most powerful technique of combat is science. What Karl Popper realized, which was genuinely revolutionary for his time, is that the criterion for a good theory isn't being true, but being falsifiable.[^49] A theory that can explain anything explains nothing: it's immune to feedback from objective reality and therefore useless. A scientific theory makes specific bets about what should happen — and if it doesn't happen, the theory loses. A good scientist doesn't seek confirmation; they seek refutation. They actively try to destroy their own idea (or someone else's). And if they can't, they trust it more — not because they proved it true, but because it survived attempts to prove it false. Literally the opposite of what the brain does by default.

Dialectics works similarly, but in the domain of language and debate.[^50] Since Socrates — who spent his time in Athens asking annoying questions to big-headed know-it-alls who thought they knew things — the idea is the same: thought is purified through friction. Alone, you build arguments that convince yourself and turn the opposing argument into a straw man (instead of a steel man), resulting in weak argumentation. In genuine dialogue, where the other person truly disagrees and presents arguments, the cracks appear. Hegel formalized this with his dialectic: the thesis (being) encounters the antithesis (non-being) and, from that conflict, the synthesis (becoming) is produced — always richer than either side alone, generating something neither could see before. Civilized debate — unlike the kind where a politician hurls a chair at another — is a machine for expanding our understanding of the universe.

And there's a practice that is both the simplest and the hardest of all: actively seeking out those who disagree with you and truly listening — including in your own internal thoughts. It's not about winning the debate. It's not about finding the flaw in their argument. You need to understand why someone intelligent, with access to the same information, arrived at a different conclusion. Or even, how is it that someone who seems so dumb and stupid and wrong is still alive today? There must be something you're not seeing. This requires what the Stoics called control of the impulse to assent — the ability to receive an impression without immediately granting or denying it credit, without judgment, letting Kahneman's slow system take the field. It's uncomfortable, but it's a sign you've left the bubble.

In short, on the metaphorical battlefield — where the audience is ignorant and our opponent is delusion — these techniques arm us to fight in the name of truth. Unfortunately, they're not laser beams. The common, tragic point between all these techniques is that none of them completely eliminates confirmation bias or delusion. But we can go into battle with confidence and beat the hell out of that guy. What these practices do, at minimum, is create enough friction that the madman doesn't win by forfeit. They make confrontation with reality harder to avoid. And sometimes that's enough — because objective reality, as we've seen, doesn't disappear just because you closed your eyes or threw rubber ducks at it. Sooner or later, the deluded will encounter the black swan and smash their face against the hard truth.

### _**Temporal paradoxes**_

Curiously and unfortunately, it's important to think about time. After all, if we make a claim, implicitly that claim is true within a period of time. In the way verbs work, for example in English, they come loaded with a specific tense (definite or indefinite).

However, if you stop to think, everything that's in the future isn't true yet, nor does it exist; it's not part of reality. And what's in the present isn't immediately verified; it's the pure action of cause, and the effect produces the contrast and change. Thus, we can assert that truth only exists in the past. History is true, and only history. What happened happened, and only what happened happened. Therefore, it's impossible to assert: "I will get better." Ha.

A thought I'd like to leave is that, since truth lives in the past, it fatefully doesn't exist in complete reality.[^51] Truth represents only a memory. So if we're being pedantic, saying that I enjoy dressing up as a femboy or that one day I'll love dressing up as a femboy is always illogical — after all, I could only claim things that have already happened, meaning I could only say I _used to_ enjoy dressing up as a femboy. Thing of the past.

The truth that exists in the future is merely a prediction based on what we experienced in the past. The future isn't defined, but we can imagine that the immediate future tends not to stray too far from the immediate past — since instants are continuous and reality doesn't jump (otherwise you'd blink and one arm would be on Jupiter and your leg at the left edge of Orion's Belt, which doesn't happen; your arm and leg generally stay close to where they are in the next instant). This is why our models work reasonably well in the short term: we're interpolating between past truths, not truly predicting the future.

An example: if and only if Pinocchio lies, his nose grows, and then he says: "My nose will grow!" We immediately find ourselves in a temporal paradox of truth. If he's telling the truth, his sentence implies his nose won't grow; but if it doesn't grow, in reality he's lying! If he's lying, his sentence implies his nose will grow; but if it grows, in reality he's telling the truth![^52] From this, we can deductively assert that a device that perfectly predicts the future is impossible. The domain of truth doesn't reach the future.

And when we talk about the present, it's even more slippery, but Einstein offers us a structural reason for this.[^53] In the theory of relativity, space and time are not independent dimensions: they're faces of the same fabric — spacetime. What this implies is that "when" something happens is not a property of the event; it depends on the observer's frame of reference. Two observers in relative motion can genuinely disagree about the order in which two events occurred, and both can be correct in their respective frames. Temporal truth, therefore, depends on the scope of observers — exactly as we've already established. There is no universal "now" that everyone shares simultaneously.

The present, in this sense, is not an objective instant of the universe. It's a local construction, valid in a specific frame of reference. It's also an illusion. The act of transforming a future instant into a past one. Adding to the argument, the existence of the present is paradoxical: for the truth of the future instant to exist in the present, it must destroy the truth of the present instant and take its place — but for this to happen, both instants would need to be present at the same time so one can destroy the other, which is impossible. When we speak of truth in the present, we're referring to an infinitesimal relationship between truth in the past and its consistency in the future.

An example: congratulating someone for having survived today. We immediately find ourselves in a temporal paradox of truth. If we say this to someone at 11:59 PM, we can't guarantee they'll survive today — after all, the day isn't over yet and they might not survive the last minute! If we say it at 12:00 AM, it's no longer the previous day; so "today" ends up referring to the day that just started, which is even worse! In other words, it's impossible to make a claim about the entirety of the present day. The domain of truth ends in the past tense.

And this reveals something curious (brace yourself for the abstraction roller coaster): as we've already shown, perfectly predicting the future and asserting about the present with total precision are impossible acts within a temporal framework. We originally took time as a human illusion that helps us understand the universe. But we see in quantum physics the act of observing — of registering a decision — and this act does something very specific: it transforms a possibility into a fact. Before observation, the system is in superposition; after, it's defined. Before: present; after: past. And if we look closely at this, we realize that this is exactly what time does. Time is not a neutral dimension where things simply happen — time is the process by which truth is created. To observe is to create truth. To create truth is to create past. And to create past is what we experience as the passage of time. Thus, we can say that time truly exists, but it has only two dimensions: that of truth (past) and that of possibility (present and future). Everything that exists has already been observed; everything that can exist has not yet been.

Fatefully, this concept is rooted in human nature at an unparalleled depth. If we pause to analyze the direction of time in the physical sense — why does time move toward the future and not the past? — we return to our premise of entropy: the vector of the universe's time points in the direction of greater disorder.[^54] But we can analyze this truth in a subjective reality; there's a curious phenomenon in our subjective perception of time: it passes faster when we're learning something new, creating something that didn't exist, building a skill, solving a difficult problem, connecting with someone for real, doing something fun that we love. But then again, time also flies when you're doomscrolling TikTok at 3 AM, and nobody's going to argue that's good for you — but your brain runs on heuristics, and when bombarded with information it believes it's doing something worthwhile, and that's one of our limitations. So it's on you to reflect.

What compresses subjective time is specific: it's the acts in which you walk against entropy — or believe you're walking against it. Learning is organizing chaos into pattern. Creating is imposing structure on the void. Connecting is synchronizing two subjective realities. In all these activities, the direction of your subjective time points in the opposite direction from the universe's. The universe marches toward disorder. You, when you're being most human, march in the opposite direction. We are the contrast!

But seeing this isn't enough. You can perfectly understand that your nature points against chaos and still do nothing. Truth illuminates the path. But illuminating isn't walking. For that, we need something else.
